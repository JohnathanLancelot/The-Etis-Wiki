Etis' Home Planet & Habitat

Abiotic Factors

Although the Etis can now be found on various planets - courtesy of their stowaway tendencies - they are native to a small, rocky planet with a relatively thin atmosphere known as NEO-433 or "The Nest."

The Nest is arrid, but surface (land) temperatures are surprisingly hospitable, ranging between 5°C and 55°C. Water temperatures on the other hand, dictated by volcanic activity deep underground, can range from -10°C and 130°C. Water is notably 'heavier' on this planet than it is on Earth, but is still drinkable by humans. The air, however, does not contain enough oxygen in it to sustain human life, and so oxygen tanks are needed for human researchers on this planet.

Lighting conditions on The Nest are quite unique, with nights lasting approximately 20-32 hours and days lasting only a few (3-15) minutes. During the 'day,' the light is quite intense, and eye damage can occur to human researchers if protective gear is not used. Curiously, while Etis have been shown to see incredibly well in the dark, The Nest is rarely completely dark - pitch darkness only lasts 2-3 hours, with the rest of the night dimly lit by the planet's four moons.

This rocky planet is speckled with craters of various sizes - some ancient, others formed as recently as 4000 years ago. Mountains, valleys, and intricate cave systems and crevasses give the planet an unnerving, almost veiny appearance from space. Due to the extreme risk of predation from Etis, which will not hesitate to target a human, researchers are strictly forbidden from venturing into valleys or caves.

What little water exists on this planet (rain is light and infrequent - approximately occuring once a month), usually is found at the bottoms of very deep crevasses in the rock. Wells have been built around every research center, but researchers are strongly advised to bring water to this planet for their own use, and to stock up the research center's limited supplies. Lakes and ponds exist in a few, well-hidden locations, but are typically small and heavily guarded by Etis, which frequently rely on bodies of water to lure prey out of hiding.

Flora & Mycoflora

The Nest's low oxygen levels can reasonably be attributed to the planet's relatively sparse vegetation. While a handful of small, unexplored forests are believed to exist further south - the areas studied so far contain very little plant life. Mycoflora, on the other hand, appears to be abundant anywhere there is water.

Following are some of the species currently being studied:

  • Lucyblue - A vivid blue, lily-like flower, commonly found in valleys. Much like Earth's lilies, they are highly toxic, yet breathtakingly beautiful. In extremely small quantities, sap from their stems acts as a powerful painkiller in humans, and other potential medicinal uses are currently being explored.
  • A dreamer's toast - A fungus whose fruiting body resembles a loaf of sliced, light brown bread. While this story hasn't been verified, it is rumoured that this fungus earned its name after an inebriated researcher ate a piece, and propmtly fell into a coma from which he didn't awake for three months.
  • Etis kiss - A flower whose petals are black nearest the stem, and speckled grey further up toward their tips. These flowers commonly grow on sprawling vines along the walls of crevasses and gorges, and have the upright carriages of tulips, but are far more pointy in shape. It is theorised that Etis themselves pollinate these flowers with their fur as they move through the narrow passages in the rock, and brush up against these blooms in the process.
  • Duckberry grasses - Low to the ground, grey in colour, soft to the touch and unassuming to the eye. Duckberry grasses aren't rare so much as difficult to spot. Their flowers, little more than 5mm across, and off-write, are just as easy to miss. However, during certain stages of these plants' growth cycle, they will produce brown, date-sized berries that are edible not only to the planet's native animals, but to humans as well. They are described as having a distinctive meaty taste, much like roast duck.
  • Dragon's breath - Black flowers streaked with flares of deep purple. 'Dragon's breath' tends to grow near volcanic vents. These flowers, each measuring around 6cm in diameter, are incredibly resistant to damage from heat and fire. In fact, they have a tendency to pop up (even in non-volcanic areas) days after a fire has broken out, and appear to require next to no water at all. Very little is yet known about how these flowers are pollinated, or how their seeds are spread, however it has been found that immense heat is required for their seeds to germinate.
  • Bleeders - A bizarre, crimson-red algae-like organism appearing on the walls of some crevasses and gorges, as well as on some flat exanses of rock; particularly in valleys. The idea that 'bleeders' appear in an area shortly after an Etis has made a kill there, is likely a myth, but they can certainly look alarming to anyone who isn't accustomed to seeing them.
  • Alyss bands - Thick vines bearing razor-sharp thorns and tiny pastel pink flowers. Alyss bands are frequently seen near the nesting places of Etis, and they appear to have a mutualistic relationship with them. While their thorns offer the Etis young an extra layer of protection as they grow, they themselves are nourished by the blood and decaying scraps of flesh left over from the young animals' messy meals.

Interestingly enough, all flowers on this planet close up during daylight hours - perhaps to limit damage to their sensitive reproductive structures.

Fauna

It is commonly remarked, by researchers new to this planet, that The Nest looks void of life, though it takes only one night at the center for a person's mind to change. Despite the best efforts of staff, alarmingly large, yet generally harmless, insect-like life forms frequently make it into the bunkrooms. These creatures range in size from 5cm in length, to 12cm, and can flatten themselves enough to squeeze through gaps as thin as a standard pencil.

Larger, furred creatures also inhabit this planet, but are extremely timid and difficult to spot. 'Eddies' are quite common - looking at first glance incredibly similar to juvenile Etis due to their grey body fur, similar body shape, and off-white furred faces (mimicing bone). Their shrill, human-like screams and piercing green eyes make them quite intimidating, but in reality they have never been known to attack a researcher. They feed exclusively on small insects and vegetation, and are believed to have evolved to mimic female Etis for their own safety. The biggest giveaway (up close) that you are looking at an Eddie and not an Etis, is their feet. Eddies have cloven feet, while Etis have almost reptillian- looking back feet, and front feet that look like hands.

A variety of omnivorous species ranging in size from that of cats to that of medium-sized dogs can occasionally be spotted just before, just after, and during the short periods of daylight on this planet. These creatures blend incredibly well into the rocks around them - and as many have striped grey fur, many research teams collectively refer to them as 'tabbies.' They make up the bulk of the Etis' diet.

Flighted creatures, nicknamed 'nest bats,' dominate the skies, along with a few winged species of the insect-like group of organisms found here. Curiously though, these creatures only appear during one specific period of the day-night cycle - that is, 5 hours to 10 minutes before the light comes. Researchers have yet to determine with certainty why this is the case, but one theory suggests that it is because the flighted 'insects' carry toxins in their soft tissues; toxins which have been shown to break down rapidly when the animal is exposed to sunlight. Nest bats are immune to this toxin, and feed almost exclusively on these insects. It is possible that, even when sheltering from the light, some light gets to these insects and damages these toxins, leaving them more vulnerable to a range of other predators. Coming out after as much time as possible has passed since the last light, potentially allows these insects to build this chemical defence back up in their bodies before risking an appearance. Naturally, nest bats tend to be out and active when their primary source of food is.
